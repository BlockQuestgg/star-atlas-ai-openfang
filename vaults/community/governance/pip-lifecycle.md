# PIP Lifecycle — Polity Improvement Proposals

Polity Improvement Proposals (PIPs) are the formal mechanism through which the Star Atlas community proposes and votes on changes to the metaverse. They provide a structured process for proposing new features, processes, or experience changes.

## What Is a PIP

A PIP is a formal document that:

- Describes a proposed change to the Star Atlas ecosystem
- Provides detailed context and rationale
- Documents community sentiment and dissenting opinions
- Goes through a structured lifecycle before being voted on

PIP authors are responsible for building consensus within the community and shepherding their proposal through the process.

## PIP Lifecycle Phases

### Phase 1: Declaration

The PIP is formally published and submitted to the Star Atlas DAO through the governance portal at [govern.staratlas.com](https://govern.staratlas.com/). The author presents the proposal with full documentation including the problem statement, proposed solution, and expected impact.

### Phase 2: Deliberation

During deliberation, the community engages with the proposal:

- DAO members submit comments and questions
- Non-binding **Public Interest Voting** gauges community sentiment
- At least one **live debate session** is scheduled for real-time discussion
- The PIP author may revise the proposal based on feedback
- This phase ensures proposals are thoroughly examined before formal voting

### Phase 3: Voting

PVP holders (those with locked POLIS — see [[dao-overview]]) cast their formal, binding votes:

- Voting power is proportional to locked POLIS × remaining lock duration
- Votes are recorded on-chain for transparency
- A proposal must meet defined quorum and approval thresholds to pass

### Phase 4: Resolution

Based on the vote outcome:

- **Passed** — the proposal is approved for implementation
- **Failed** — the proposal is rejected; it may be revised and resubmitted

## PIP Categories

PIPs can address a wide range of topics:

- Game feature additions or changes
- Economic parameter adjustments (fees, rewards, sinks)
- Treasury spending proposals
- Protocol and governance upgrades
- Community initiatives and partnerships

## Writing a PIP

Effective PIPs share common traits:

- Clear problem definition
- Specific, actionable proposed changes
- Impact analysis — who benefits, who is affected
- Risk assessment — trade-offs and potential unintended consequences
- Historical context — relevant precedents and prior proposals
